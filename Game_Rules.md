### ## Foundational Objective
The primary goal of Dehla Pakad is to "catch" as many **10s** (known as *Dehlas* or *Mindis*) as possible in your team's tricks. While there are 13 tricks in a round, winning the tricks themselves is secondary to winning the tricks that contain the four 10s.

*   **Teams:** The game is played by 4 players in two fixed partnerships (sitting across from each other).
*   **The Deck:** A standard 52-card full deck is used.
---

### ## The Deal and Trump Selection
The dealing process and trump selection are unique and vary slightly by region. Typically:

1.  **Dealing:** The dealer distributes the cards in batches (e.g., 4 cards to each player first, then one card each until the deck is empty).
2.  **Choosing Trump (Hukum): The player to the dealer's right looks at their first four cards and selects a trump suit and announce it to everyone

---

### ## Gameplay Mechanics
The gameplay follows standard trick-taking rules with a few specific twists:

*   **Following Suit:** Players must follow the suit led if possible. If they don't have a card of that suit, they can play a trump card or any other card.
*   **Winning a Trick:** The highest card of the suit led wins the trick, unless a trump is played, in which case the highest trump wins.

---

### ## Scoring and Winning
The score is determined by the number of 10s captured by each team:

*   **Winning the Hand:** A team wins if they capture **three or four 10s**.
*   **Mindi/Dehla:** If a team captures all four 10s, they have performed a "Mindi" or "Dehla Pakad," which is a major victory.
*   **Kot (Whitewash):** If one team wins all the 10s in several consecutive hands (usually 7), it is called a "Kot," representing a total defeat for the opponents.
*   **Winner on Draw Condition:** If both team wins two 10s then winner decides by the highest tricks won by a team
---

### ## Key Strategic Concepts
To excel at Dehla Pakad, players must move beyond simply playing high cards and focus on these tactical elements:

*   **10-Protection:** Since the 10 is the most valuable card but is lower than the Jack, Queen, King, and Ace, you must be careful not to play it unless you are certain your partner will win the trick or the higher cards in that suit have already been played.
*   **Trump Management:** Identifying when to play the trump suit and using low trumps to steal tricks containing an opponent's 10 is the hallmark of an experienced player.
*   **Memory:** Tracking which 10s have been played and which high cards (Aces/Kings) remain is essential to ensure your 10s aren't "caught" by the opposing team.

---

### ## Common Confusion Points
*   **Ranking:** Remember that the Ace is the highest card, followed by King, Queen, Jack, and then the 10. The 10 is the "prize," but it is not the strongest card in terms of winning the trick.
